Your resume is your first impression — and in most cases, it's screened by an Applicant Tracking System (ATS) before a human ever reads it. Here's how to write one that passes both filters.

## Start With a Strong Header

Your header should include your full name, phone number, email, LinkedIn URL, and GitHub (if applicable). Skip the physical address — it's outdated and wastes space.

**Good example:**
```
John Doe | john@email.com | (555) 123-4567 | linkedin.com/in/johndoe | github.com/johndoe
```

## Write Impact-Driven Bullet Points

The biggest mistake job seekers make is writing responsibility-based bullets. Instead, use this formula:

> **Action Verb + What You Did + Quantified Result**

**Bad:** "Responsible for managing the database"

**Good:** "Optimized PostgreSQL query performance, reducing average response time by 40% across 12 API endpoints serving 50K daily requests"

## Choose the Right Format

For most tech roles, a single-column format works best:

1. **Header** — Name, contact info, links
2. **Experience** — Reverse chronological, 2-4 bullets per role
3. **Projects** — 2-3 relevant projects with tech stacks
4. **Skills** — Languages, frameworks, tools, databases
5. **Education** — Degree, institution, dates

## ATS Optimization Tips

- Use standard section headings ("Experience", not "Where I've Worked")
- Include keywords from the job description naturally
- Avoid tables, columns, and graphics — ATS can't parse them
- Save as PDF unless the application specifically asks for .docx
- Use a clean, professional font like Inter or Calibri

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Generic objectives** — Replace with a targeted summary or skip entirely
2. **Listing every technology** — Focus on what's relevant to the role
3. **Typos** — Have someone else proofread. Seriously.
4. **Too long** — One page for &lt;10 years experience, two pages max
5. **No metrics** — If you can't quantify, at least qualify the impact
